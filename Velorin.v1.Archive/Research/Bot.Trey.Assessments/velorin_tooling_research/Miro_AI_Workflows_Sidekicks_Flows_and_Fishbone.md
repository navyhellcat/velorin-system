# Miro_AI_Workflows_Sidekicks_Flows_and_Fishbone

**Date:** March 28, 2026  
**Purpose:** Clarify what Miro's current AI workflow product actually is, resolve likely ambiguity around "Miro fish," and assess relevance to Velorin.

## Executive Summary

There does not appear to be a major current Miro product called **"Miro Fish."** The likely interpretations are:
1. **Miro AI Workflows / Sidekicks / Flows**, or
2. Miro's **fishbone diagram** tooling/templates.

Those are very different things. The first is an AI workflow layer. The second is a structured root-cause analysis format.

For Velorin, the important part is the first one: Miro is pushing toward a **shared-canvas AI workflow environment**, where the board itself becomes the prompt and AI agents/flows operate in the context of visual work.

## What Miro Is Building

Miro positions itself as an AI platform / innovation workspace. Its current AI framing emphasizes:
- **Sidekicks** — AI teammates / agents working in the shared canvas
- **Flows** — visual AI workflows that turn board content into deliverables
- the idea that the **canvas itself becomes the prompt**

Official help pages describe:
- Sidekicks as AI agents / personal assistants within Miro,
- Flows as connected multi-step processes for turning canvas content into documents, diagrams, prototypes, and more.

## Why This Matters

This is a strong design signal:
- the future is not only chat,
- visual working memory matters,
- a shared workspace can become a live context surface,
- teams want AI embedded into process, not bolted on after the fact.

## If You Meant "Miro Fishbone"

Miro also has fishbone diagram tooling/templates. Fishbone diagrams are cause-and-effect/root-cause-analysis structures.

That matters to Velorin too, but in a narrower sense:
- process diagnosis,
- failure analysis,
- system bottleneck mapping,
- postmortems.

It is useful, but it is not the main strategic signal.

## Relevance to Velorin

### Strong relevance
- shared visual architecture mapping,
- strategy canvases,
- interdependent-box mapping,
- process diagnosis,
- turning exploratory work into repeatable workflows,
- making cross-domain system relationships visible.

### Weak relevance
- secure company operating system
- canonical data store
- automated cross-tool action layer
- deep coding or repo execution

Miro is strongest as a **visual collaboration / planning / workflow surface**, not as the system backbone.

## Best Velorin Uses

### 1. Architecture mapping
Use Miro to visualize:
- five boxes,
- agent relationships,
- tool/interoperability map,
- current-state vs future-state stack.

### 2. Decision workshops
Use Sidekicks and Flows to turn strategy boards into reusable session outputs.

### 3. Failure analysis
Use fishbone diagrams when diagnosing why a company process keeps stalling:
- slow onboarding,
- weak agent outputs,
- failed source loading,
- output packaging issues,
- revenue-engine crowdout.

### 4. Future client packaging
If Velorin needs to show system architecture visually, Miro is a plausible working surface.

## Risks

- visual theater without execution,
- overinvestment in diagrams instead of operating leverage,
- yet another workspace unless tied to actual workflows,
- governance/security may not match core operating-system requirements.

## Recommendation

Use Miro selectively:
- for visual system design,
- workflow mapping,
- problem diagnosis,
- collaborative architecture sessions.

Do not use it as a replacement for:
- governed source-of-truth docs,
- app/MCP/tool integration design,
- execution infrastructure.

## Bottom Line

If the question was "what is Miro building?" the answer is:
**context-aware AI work on a shared visual canvas.**

If the question was "what is Miro fish?" the likely answer is:
either **Miro fishbone** or a mistaken label for **Miro AI workflows**. For Velorin, the high-value lesson is the canvas-as-context model, not the fishbone template itself.
