## Executive Summary

The empirical measurement of an autobiographical memory's emotional salience is structurally divided across three domains: self-report phenomenological scales, autonomic physiological tracking, and direct neuroimaging. The literature demonstrates a severe and persistent dissociation between a subject's introspective perception of emotional intensity and their measurable physiological arousal, establishing that self-report alone is mathematically insufficient for assigning an objective ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img000.png) (Affective Hamiltonian) parameter within the Velorin neural graph. Physiological signals, including pupillometry, Galvanic Skin Response (GSR), and Heart Rate Variability (HRV), provide highly accurate indices of sympathetic and parasympathetic arousal, but machine learning classification models using these signals fail precipitously unless they are strictly calibrated to a single user. To bypass the cost and immobility constraints of fMRI, recent computational models have successfully mapped superficial cortical hemodynamics, captured via functional Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (fNIRS), to deep-brain amygdala activity using Support Vector Regression, achieving prediction correlations of ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img001.png). Ultimately, because the act of prompted retrieval initiates inhibitory networks that alter a memory's intrinsic emotional charge, any valid Velorin measurement protocol must rely heavily on passive, continuous markers—such as involuntary intrusion frequency and response latency—rather than active querying.

## Part 1 — Self-Report Instruments

Psychometric instruments attempt to quantify the phenomenological experience of autobiographical memory. While these scales are highly standardized and widely deployed in clinical settings, they primarily measure the subjective narrative integration of a memory into the ego structure, rather than its raw neurological encoding strength or routing gravity within a semantic network.

### The Centrality of Event Scale (CES)

The Centrality of Event Scale (CES) was developed by Berntsen and Rubin to measure the extent to which a specific memory for a stressful or traumatic event forms a reference point for personal identity and the attribution of meaning to other experiences.1

What the CES Actually Measures:

The CES does not strictly measure emotional arousal, biological stress, or sympathetic nervous system activation. Instead, it measures narrative anchoring. Factor analyses of the CES reveal that it probes three distinct psychological constructs:

  1. The extent to which the individual perceives the event as a turning point in their overarching life story.
  2. The extent to which the event is a central, load-bearing component of their current identity.
  3. The extent to which the event acts as a reference point for everyday inferences and future expectations.3


Validation Status and Psychometric Properties: The CES is a highly validated instrument, exhibiting excellent internal consistency. The original 20-item version reports a Cronbach’s alpha of 0.94, while the abbreviated 7-item version reports an alpha of 0.88 across multiple undergraduate and clinical samples.2 The scale correlates positively and substantially with Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) symptom severity (![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img002.png)), depression (![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img003.png)), and prolonged grief.5

Known Weaknesses for Algorithmic Mapping: The primary weakness of the CES for assigning an objective ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img004.png) parameter is its acute susceptibility to narrative bias and current mood states. It measures how the subject currently integrates the memory, which fluctuates based on subsequent life events and cognitive reappraisal. Furthermore, research adapting the CES for positive events (CES-Positive) demonstrates that individuals frequently rate their most positive memories as more central to their identity than their most negative ones, even if the negative events carry a higher biological stress load.6 The scale also shows a consistent, albeit weak, positive correlation with the personality trait of Neuroticism, indicating that general negative affectivity inflates the perceived centrality of events independent of the event's actual historical magnitude.7 Therefore, the CES measures a memory's "Semantic Dark Matter" ignition threshold—how deeply it is woven into the user's conscious narrative—rather than its raw thermodynamic heat.

### The Impact of Event Scale-Revised (IES-R)

The IES-R is a 22-item self-report measure originally aligned with DSM-IV criteria for PTSD, designed to assess the subjective distress caused by a specific, identifiable traumatic event over the preceding seven days.8 It builds upon the original 15-item IES by incorporating hyperarousal symptoms.9

Usage Outside PTSD Contexts: While a total score of 33 or above represents the optimal cutoff for a probable PTSD diagnosis, the IES-R is routinely deployed in non-clinical and sub-clinical settings to assess general emotional disruption, acute stress reactions, and memory salience following significant life events.8 The instrument measures three distinct sub-domains:

  1. Intrusion: Assesses unbidden thoughts, involuntary recall, intrusive images, and nightmares.
  2. Avoidance: Measures deliberate, effortful attempts to suppress thoughts, avoid situational reminders, and emotional numbing.
  3. Hyperarousal: Quantifies heightened physiological reactivity, irritability, concentration deficits, and exaggerated startle responses.8


Validation and Application: The IES-R demonstrates strong construct validity and excellent internal consistency across multiple populations, including survivors of motor vehicle accidents and non-clinical undergraduates experiencing acute stressors.10 Factor analytic studies consistently support the three-factor structure.8 For the purpose of non-clinical autobiographical memory measurement, the Intrusion subscale is particularly valuable; it provides a direct, albeit subjective, metric of how aggressively a memory trace bypasses executive control to force itself into working memory. This serves as a highly relevant proxy for involuntary routing gravity within a neural file graph.

### The Memory Experiences Questionnaire (MEQ) and Autobiographical Memory Questionnaire (AMQ)

To address the need for a comprehensive assessment of memory phenomenology, researchers developed the Memory Experiences Questionnaire (MEQ) and the Autobiographical Memory Questionnaire (AMQ).

Structure and Dimensions: The MEQ is a 63-item scale designed to isolate the phenomenological qualities of remembering into ten distinct dimensions: Vividness, Coherence, Accessibility, Time Perspective, Sensory Details, Visual Perspective, Emotional Intensity, Sharing, Distancing, and Valence.12 A validated short form (MEQ-SF) reduces the item count while maintaining an acceptable median internal consistency (![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img005.png)).13 The AMQ similarly isolates parameters such as the sensation of reliving the event, belief in the memory's accuracy, spatial context, and emotional intensity.15

Emotional Intensity vs. Valence: A critical contribution of these instruments to measurement science is their structural decoupling of Valence (the positive or negative polarity of the emotion) from Intensity (the magnitude of physiological and psychological arousal). Empirical data generated using the AMQ confirms that Emotional Intensity, rather than Valence, is the primary predictor of memory vividness, the retention of sensory detail, and long-term memory persistence.15 Models that treat valence and intensity as functionally equivalent (e.g., the vector model) map more accurately to memory recall performance than circumplex models that treat valence as a bipolar continuum.17

### The Fundamental Weaknesses of Self-Report

The critical vulnerability across all self-report instruments is the severe limitation of introspective accuracy. Empirical evidence demonstrates a profound disconnect between subjective evaluations of emotional magnitude and objective physiological activation.

Studies pairing self-report questionnaires (e.g., the SUPIN S30) with continuous Galvanic Skin Response (GSR) monitoring find no systematic correlation between the reported magnitude of negative affect and the objective number of electrodermal peaks.18 Research indicates that patients can accurately report the direction of physical symptoms—acknowledging an increase in heart rate or the onset of sweating—but utterly fail to quantify the degree or absolute magnitude of those autonomic changes.20

Self-report scales are heavily confounded by social desirability bias, laboratory anxiety, and varying baselines of emotional self-awareness (alexithymia).18 Consequently, self-report scales cannot operate as the sole mechanism for mapping the Affective Hamiltonian (![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img006.png)). The human executive function is biologically blind to its own exact autonomic parameters, meaning self-report measures narrative belief rather than thermodynamic reality.

### Encoding vs. Recall and the Fading Affect Bias (FAB)

Researchers account for the difference between emotional charge at the time of encoding versus the charge during present-day recall by treating autobiographical memory as a reconstructive, rather than reproductive, process.

The Fading Affect Bias (FAB): Longitudinal measurement has established the Fading Affect Bias (FAB), a psychological phenomenon in which the emotional intensity associated with negative autobiographical memories decays at a significantly faster rate than the intensity associated with positive memories.23 The FAB acts as a healthy cognitive regulatory mechanism, supporting a positive view of the self and preventing the accumulation of paralyzing distress.

However, the FAB is heavily moderated by clinical and personality factors. Individuals experiencing dysphoria or clinical depression exhibit a significantly attenuated FAB; they hold onto the negative affective charge of past events much longer than healthy baselines.23

Reconstructive Contamination: The emotions experienced during memory retrieval often reflect the subject's current emotional goals, mood-congruent biases, and physiological state rather than a strict playback of the encoding event.26 For example, recalling a stressful event after an extended period of rest or psychological distance will yield a lower self-reported intensity than recalling the same event while currently under stress. Therefore, attempting to measure the historical ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img007.png) of a node solely through present-day recall introduces massive temporal and state-dependent contamination.

## Part 2 — Physiological and Behavioral Signals

Because introspective accuracy fails at quantifying absolute magnitude, measurement science relies heavily on the autonomic nervous system (ANS) as an objective proxy for emotional memory strength. These signals index the metabolic and electrical changes that accompany memory activation, bypassing executive filtering.

### Galvanic Skin Response (GSR) / Electrodermal Activity (EDA)

Galvanic Skin Response (GSR), or Electrodermal Activity (EDA), measures the electrical conductance of the skin, which varies strictly with the moisture level produced by eccrine sweat glands.

Validation and Application: GSR is a highly validated, independent index of sympathetic nervous system (SNS) activity.27 The activation of sweat glands is triggered autonomously by emotional stimulation; it cannot be consciously fabricated or perfectly suppressed. In behavioral research, GSR is utilized to gauge emotional arousal levels, stress, and excitement. During autobiographical memory recall, an increase in GSR amplitude and the frequency of electrodermal peaks reliably correlate with the retrieval of emotionally intense memories compared to neutral cognitive tasks (e.g., counting or reading neutral text).18

Effect Sizes and Limitations: While GSR is acutely sensitive to the magnitude of arousal, it is completely blind to emotional valence. A subject experiencing sheer terror, intense anger, or extreme joy will produce virtually identical electrodermal spikes.31 Furthermore, as noted in Part 1, GSR frequently diverges from self-reported emotional intensity.19 Therefore, GSR is an excellent metric for determining the absolute value of ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img008.png), but it cannot determine whether that charge is aversive or appetitive without supplementary data.

### Pupillometry (Pupil Dilation)

Pupil dilation reflects the noradrenergic activity of the autonomic nervous system. The locus coeruleus-norepinephrine (LC-NE) system, which regulates arousal and attention, directly innervates the dilator pupillae muscle.27

Validation for Emotional Memory: Pupil size is a validated, real-time biomarker for cognitive load, affective attention, and memory processing. The retrieval of autobiographical memory—regardless of whether the memory is positive or negative—yields significantly larger pupil diameters than baseline cognitive tasks.33 Crucially, pupillometry captures temporal variations during the memory cycle; words or images that are successfully encoded and later recalled are associated with greater pupil dilation during the encoding phase than items that are subsequently forgotten.34

Control Requirements and Predictive Power: Pupillary response is highly sensitive to extraneous variables, particularly ambient luminance and the spatial frequency (visual complexity) of stimuli. However, when these visual parameters are strictly controlled in a laboratory setting, machine learning algorithms utilizing dynamic time warping-based clustering can achieve high classification accuracy. Models focusing on features such as the area under the pupil dilation curve can classify emotional states (distinguishing between high/low arousal and positive/negative valence) with approximately 79% accuracy and an Area Under the Curve (AUC) of 0.88.36

### Heart Rate Variability (HRV)

Heart Rate Variability (HRV) indexes the fluctuation in the precise time intervals between adjacent heartbeats. It serves as the primary indicator of parasympathetic nervous system (PNS) regulation, mediated via the vagus nerve.

Validation: Higher resting HRV, particularly in the high-frequency (HF) spectrum, correlates directly with superior emotion regulation capacity and enhanced functional connectivity between the medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) and the amygdala.37 During acute stress or high cognitive load (such as the difficult retrieval of a traumatic memory), HRV reliably decreases while the base heart rate accelerates.39 Experimental inductions of high-amplitude HRV oscillations (e.g., through resonance breathing biofeedback) have been shown to directly alter emotional memory biases, increasing the recall of positive items relative to negative ones by entraining regulatory brain networks.37

### The Subject-Dependency Classification Problem

A fundamental barrier exists in translating raw physiological signals into generalized emotional metrics. When researchers fuse multimodal signals (GSR, pupillometry, HRV, EEG) using Support Vector Machines (SVM) or Deep Neural Networks to classify emotional load, they encounter a severe subject-dependency bottleneck.

If an algorithm is trained on a dataset that includes data from the target subject (subject-dependent training, such as standard 10-fold cross-validation), classification accuracy for discrete emotional states routinely reaches 75% to 86%.41 However, when researchers attempt "leave-one-subject-out" cross-validation—training the model on a group of individuals and testing it on a completely unseen subject—the classification accuracy collapses to approximately 32%, which is barely better than random guessing for a four-emotion classification task.41

Velorin Connection: This literature finding provides total empirical validation for the Velorin architecture's single-user constraint. Generalized, zero-shot physiological classification is biologically unviable because baseline autonomic tone, skin thickness, baseline pupil diameter, and resting heart rate vary too widely across the population. Any system calculating an accurate ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img009.png) parameter must be individually calibrated to the specific user's autonomic baseline.

### Behavioral Signals: Response Latency and Involuntary Intrusions

Response Latency: Response latency (reaction time) is frequently used as a proxy for the ease of node activation in memory networks. The literature yields highly complex results regarding emotional charge. While highly salient, emotionally charged oddball stimuli are often recognized faster and remembered better, the recall of intense autobiographical memories can introduce proactive interference.43 Because emotional memories are densely connected to autonomic states and other related affective memories, cueing an emotional node can activate competing memory traces, which actually slows down working memory performance and increases response latency.43 Consequently, as a standalone proxy for ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img010.png), raw latency is noisy and unreliable unless it is isolated to pure, distractor-free recognition tasks.

Involuntary Recall (Intrusion Frequency): Involuntary memories are explicit memories of personal events that burst into conscious awareness without any preceding executive attempt at retrieval.46 The frequency of these unbidden intrusions serves as the cleanest, most mathematically pure behavioral proxy for emotional charge in the literature.

Validation: Intrusive memory frequency is directly driven by the absolute magnitude of peritraumatic emotional arousal at encoding; events that trigger massive sympathetic spikes produce traces that spontaneously reactivate for days or weeks.47 Unlike voluntary memory search, daydreaming, or directed future-thinking, the frequency of involuntary autobiographical remembering does not naturally decline with age, highlighting its distinct neurobiological mechanism.49 Because involuntary intrusions bypass the prefrontal cortex's executive filtering, counting the frequency of spontaneous trace activations provides an unadulterated index of a node's independent routing gravity.

## Part 3 — Neuroimaging Correlates

The biological mechanism determining whether a memory persists for decades or decays within weeks is centered in the medial temporal lobe (MTL), specifically governed by the amygdala and its dynamic coupling with other brain structures.

### Amygdala Activation at Encoding

The basolateral complex of the amygdala (BLA) serves as the biological equivalent of the assignment mechanism for the Affective Hamiltonian (![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img011.png)).

Replication and Predictive Power: Decades of functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) and Positron Emission Tomography (PET) studies provide overwhelming consensus that amygdala activation at the time of encoding predicts long-term retention. Glucose metabolic rate and Blood-Oxygen-Level-Dependent (BOLD) signals in the right amygdala during the viewing of emotionally arousing stimuli are highly and positively correlated with the number of details retrieved in free recall tests conducted weeks later.51

The amygdala functions as a highly conserved threat and reward detector. When an event triggers emotional arousal, the amygdala commands the release of stress hormones (norepinephrine and cortisol). This neurochemical flood directly modulates synaptic plasticity, histone modifications, gene expression, and the translation of plasticity-associated proteins in the hippocampus.54 Crucially, the brain does not distinguish between a profound psychological emotional threat and immediate physical danger; both activate the exact same BLA-hippocampal consolidation pathways to ensure the organism retains the data necessary for future survival.55

### Hippocampal-Amygdala Coupling

The absolute activation of the amygdala is necessary but insufficient; the strength of the memory trace is ultimately determined by the functional coupling between the amygdala and the hippocampus during and immediately after the event.

Validation as a Consolidation Marker: Human intracranial electroencephalography (iEEG) recordings have revealed the precise neural dynamics of this communication. Successful emotional memory encoding depends heavily on theta oscillations (4-7 Hz) in the amygdala. Hippocampal gamma activity (>25 Hz) and neuronal firing must couple exactly to the phase of this amygdala theta rhythm.56 This phase difference enables lagged coherence between the two structures. The amygdala does not store the declarative memory itself; rather, it acts as a rhythmic amplifier, dispatching targeted signals to the hippocampus that physically warp and prioritize the memory consolidation process.57 Greater functional connectivity between these regions during encoding and subsequent sleep stages reliably predicts individual differences in the persistence of recollection over long delays.60

### Portable Alternatives to fMRI in Non-Clinical Settings

The Velorin operating system is designed for continuous, naturalistic use and cannot require an fMRI machine. Fortunately, recent advances in neurotechnology provide mobile, real-world alternatives for capturing these neuroimaging correlates.

Functional Near-Infrared Spectroscopy (fNIRS): fNIRS measures cerebral hemodynamic responses—changes in oxygenated (HbO) and deoxygenated (HbR) hemoglobin—by projecting near-infrared light (700–1100 nm) through the scalp and skull. The physiological principles are identical to those of fMRI BOLD signals, but the equipment is highly portable, cost-effective, and tolerant of motion artifacts.61

The Deep-Brain Limitation: Photons from fNIRS systems typically penetrate only 10 to 15 millimeters into cortical tissue. Consequently, deep limbic structures critical to emotional memory, such as the amygdala and hippocampus, are physically invisible to direct fNIRS measurement.62

The Computational Workaround: Because the brain operates in tightly coupled functional networks, superficial cortical activity systematically correlates with subcortical activity. Researchers have successfully mapped this relationship. By capturing simultaneous fNIRS and fMRI data during cognitive tasks, machine learning models—specifically Support Vector Regression (SVR) and Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) neural networks—can learn the mathematical mapping between the surface and the depths. Once trained, these models can take real-time fNIRS data from the prefrontal cortex and accurately infer the hidden fMRI BOLD signal in the amygdala (![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img012.png)) and the hippocampus (![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img013.png)).65 Velorin Connection: This SVR/MLP prediction architecture provides a mathematically proven, peer-reviewed blueprint for inferring deep-brain emotional arousal (the generation of ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img014.png)) using only a non-invasive, portable fNIRS headband.

Electroencephalography (EEG) and the Late Positive Potential (LPP): EEG captures electrical voltage fluctuations on the scalp with millisecond precision, offering superior temporal resolution to fNIRS.61

Validation of the LPP: The Late Positive Potential (LPP) is an event-related potential component that occurs between 300 and 1000+ milliseconds following stimulus presentation. The amplitude of the LPP is a highly reliable, heavily validated index of emotional salience, affective attention, and arousal magnitude.70 Furthermore, LPP amplitude during encoding directly predicts delayed memory consolidation success; a higher LPP amplitude mediates the resistance of negative memories to decay over time.72

The integration of EEG and fNIRS into a single wearable headband provides the optimal multimodal solution, compensating for the spatial limitations of EEG and the temporal lag of fNIRS hemodynamics.62

## Part 4 — The Measurement Problem (The Observer Effect)

A fundamental epistemological and neurobiological paradox exists in the measurement of emotional memory: observing the memory changes the memory.

### The Biological Observer Effect

Recalling an autobiographical memory does not function like retrieving a static file from a hard drive. When an episodic trace is brought into active working memory, the neural network undergoes reconsolidation. During this window, the memory trace becomes temporarily labile (unstable) and vulnerable to structural modification, interference, or updating.59

If the retrieval act is explicitly prompted by a system or researcher (e.g., asking, "How intense is this memory right now?"), the prefrontal cortex engages executive control pathways to formulate an answer. Specifically, intentional retrieval suppression, emotional regulation, or cognitive reappraisal engages a right frontoparietal network. This network exerts a top-down inhibitory control signal that simultaneously suppresses activity in both the hippocampus and the amygdala.59

The Fundamental Difficulty: It is biologically impossible to measure ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img015.png) via prompted, conscious introspection without structurally altering ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img016.png). The act of focusing executive attention on the emotional content engages the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC), which inherently dampens the amygdaloid response, reduces autonomic signals (GSR, Pupil, Heart Rate), and alters the memory's affective trace for future retrievals.59 The measurement destroys the integrity of the object being measured.

### The Trajectory of Change Over Time

Emotional memory charge is not static. The trajectory of change is measured longitudinally through repeated ecological momentary assessments (EMA) or diary studies that track intrusion frequencies and subjective ratings over weeks or years. As discussed regarding the Fading Affect Bias (FAB), the charge naturally decays, but the rate of decay is highly non-linear and heavily dependent on subsequent life events, rumination frequency, and sleep architecture (specifically the density of REM sleep and slow-wave spindle coupling, which facilitates offline consolidation).24

### Consensus on Introspective Reliability

The literature consensus is highly fractured, but converges on a stark reality: while self-report scales are reliable for measuring a subject's narrative belief about their memory, they are fundamentally unreliable for measuring the actual biological intensity or accuracy of the memory trace.77 Introspective accuracy regarding somatic and autonomic symptoms is valid only for directionality, not absolute scale.20 Consequently, an architecture cannot safely update the ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img017.png) parameter solely by asking the user to rate a memory on a scale of 1-10. The user's reported confidence in their emotional memory bears a weak and often misleading relationship to the underlying neurobiological reality.77

## Part 5 — Summary: What Would a Reliable Measurement Procedure Look Like?

Given the failure of uncalibrated biometric classification and the destructive nature of the biological observer effect, a reliable measurement procedure for assigning the Affective Hamiltonian (![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img018.png)) parameter to a Velorin neuron, without requiring fMRI, must rely on a hybrid architecture of passive behavioral capture and deep-brain algorithmic inference.

### The Velorin ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img019.png) Assignment Pipeline (Minimum Viable Architecture)

The following procedure synthesizes the validated components from the literature into an operational protocol that satisfies the constraints of the Velorin architecture.

1\. User Calibration Baseline (One-Time Setup): Because zero-shot physiological classification fails across subjects, interpretation requires a subject-specific anchor. The system must establish the Chairman's baseline variance for GSR, HF-HRV, and pupil dilation under neutral cognitive load versus acute stress. A Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifier must be trained exclusively on this specific baseline.41

2\. The Passive OS Intrusion Tracker (Primary Behavioral Signal):

Because prompting alters the memory, the system must prioritize unprompted behavior. An OS-level tracker monitors the frequency of involuntary intrusions—quantified by tracking unprompted search queries, the spontaneous manual opening of specific c-memory files, or explicitly logged spontaneous thoughts captured via a zero-friction hotkey. High intrusion frequency relative to node age directly scales the ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img020.png) parameter upward, acting as the purest index of routing gravity.

3\. Multimodal Headband Capture (Episodic Signal): During scheduled deep-work sessions, the user wears a combined fNIRS + EEG headband and an eye-tracking peripheral.35

  - LPP Extraction: EEG captures the Late Positive Potential amplitude between 300-1000ms upon node visualization, proving immediate affective attention.
  - Deep-Brain Inference: fNIRS captures prefrontal hemodynamics. A pre-trained Support Vector Regression (SVR) model maps this superficial oxygenation data to an inferred amygdala BOLD response, outputting a scalar emotional intensity value.65
  - Autonomic Confirmation: Pupil dilation and GSR peaks are logged simultaneously. If dilation and GSR spike in tandem with the SVR prediction, sympathetic arousal is confirmed and ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img021.png) is locked.


4\. Self-Report for Narrative Weight Only:

The Centrality of Event Scale (CES) 7-item short form is administered infrequently. The resulting score is explicitly not used to calculate raw arousal. Instead, it sets the "Semantic Dark Matter" ignition threshold—determining how structurally embedded the memory is in the user's conscious ego narrative, which influences how easily the node can be retrieved via standard text prompting.

Data extracted from the literature establishes that a comprehensive pipeline translates these multimodal inputs into a unified metric. Inputs from fNIRS, EEG, wearable sensors, OS event logs, and UI inputs pass through a signal extraction phase to isolate prefrontal hemodynamics, LPP amplitude, GSR/pupil peaks, intrusion counts, and CES/IES-R scores. These extracted features are then processed by the SVR amygdala prediction and subject-tuned SVM models, culminating in the final assignment of the ![](images/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement/Trey.Research.EmotionalMemorySalience.Measurement_img022.png) parameter for the corresponding Velorin neuron.
