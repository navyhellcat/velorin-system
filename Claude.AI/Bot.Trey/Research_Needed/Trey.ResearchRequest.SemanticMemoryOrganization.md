# Research Request: Neuroscience of Semantic Memory Organization

## Context

I am designing a knowledge graph for an AI personal operating system. The graph
needs to be organized into Regions and Areas — top-level and mid-level categories
that group related concepts. The mathematical architecture is now complete (PPR
retrieval, E₈ crystal storage, Simon-Ando macro-regions, LoRa semantic layer).
The open question is whether cognitive neuroscience provides a principled
organizational taxonomy for how conceptual knowledge should be partitioned and indexed
in the Brain — or whether that structure should emerge bottom-up from the data.

The question is NOT: what are the anatomical regions of the brain?
The question IS: what does cognitive neuroscience say about how semantic knowledge
naturally clusters in functional terms, and could that organization inform the
design of an AI knowledge graph intended to model one specific human's cognition?

## Specific Research Questions

**Q1:** The semantic memory literature (temporal-parietal region, angular gyrus,
anterior temporal lobe as semantic hub) — does it identify natural knowledge categories,
or does it only describe the processing infrastructure? Is there peer-reviewed evidence
that certain conceptual domains (abstract vs concrete, social vs physical, biological
vs artifact, etc.) cluster distinctly in functional terms during semantic retrieval?

**Q2:** Hub-and-Spoke architecture for semantic memory: the anterior temporal lobe (ATL)
as a "semantic hub" that converges information from modality-specific "spoke" areas.
Does this architecture suggest a principled way to organize a knowledge graph — e.g.,
hub-concepts that bridge many domains vs spoke-concepts that are modality-specific?
Are there experimental papers that operationalize the Hub-and-Spoke distinction
in ways that could inform a graph organizational taxonomy?

**Q3:** Individual differences in semantic memory organization. Is there evidence
that different people organize the same knowledge differently (by expertise, experience,
cognitive style)? What determines individual variation in semantic organization?
This is highly relevant: Velorin is building a user-specific knowledge graph for
one person (Christian Taylor, WAIS-IV FSIQ 133, ENTP-A, five-domain life structure).
If individual semantic organization varies meaningfully, a person-specific Brain
should reflect their specific organizational structure, not a universal taxonomy.

**Q4:** Content categories vs. functional categories. Does the brain organize knowledge
by WHAT it is (finance, health, relationships, AI) or by HOW it is processed
(procedural vs declarative, episodic vs semantic, concrete vs abstract)?
Or does it maintain parallel organizational axes simultaneously?

**Q5:** Published frameworks from cognitive architecture research (ACT-R, SOAR,
Global Workspace Theory, Integrated Information Theory) that propose principled
taxonomies for how an AI system should organize conceptual knowledge based on how
human memory works. Any AI knowledge representation framework that draws directly
from cognitive science to justify its organizational structure.

**Discovery directive:** Beyond these specific questions, find what the field knows
about semantic memory organization that I haven't named in this prompt. What would
a cognitive neuroscientist say is the most important thing someone building an
AI knowledge graph should understand about how conceptual knowledge organizes in
the human mind?

## Deliverable

A research document (Jiang output format: research pathway, confidence ratings,
official conclusions, open questions) that gives me enough to determine:

(a) Whether neuroscience provides a principled content-level taxonomy that should
    seed the Velorin Brain's Region/Area structure, OR
(b) Whether the neuroscience only describes processing infrastructure (not content
    organization), in which case the Brain's regions should emerge bottom-up from
    the user's actual knowledge through Simon-Ando clustering.

Either conclusion is valid and useful. Do not force an answer if the evidence
does not support one. Confidence-rate every conclusion.
