CORRECTION: THE 50% BASELINE
The first version of this document treated 0-100 as a linear scale where 0 meant zero discrimination and 100 meant perfect discrimination. That was wrong.
The Turing Vault is a binary classification task. Every question has two options: human or AI. A person selecting randomly — eyes closed, coin flip — will score 50 on average. Fifty is not average performance. Fifty is zero performance. It is the mathematical floor of the instrument.
This means the effective measurement range is 50 to 100. That is a 50-point window. Everything below 50 is noise, inversion, or non-engagement. It cannot be treated as a gradient of meaningful discrimination.
This revision corrects the entire framework. Score bands are redrawn against the 50-100 effective range. Everything below 50 collapses into a single band. The copy, the risk framework, and the resource architecture are rebuilt accordingly. Parts that were structurally unaffected — toggle logic, implementation schema, override copy — are preserved from v1.

THE VERBAL FLUENCY THESIS
This section was missing from v1. It is the theoretical backbone of the entire results page and the reason the resource ladder is structured the way it is. It also belongs on the landing page.
The Core Argument
The Turing Vault measures verbal discrimination: the ability to distinguish human-generated text from AI-generated text under controlled conditions. This is not a parlor trick. It is a proxy for a cluster of cognitive capabilities that determine how a person will fare in an AI-transformed economy.
Verbal fluency — the broader trait that verbal discrimination correlates with — predicts abstract reasoning, creative recombination, and the capacity to think non-linearly. These are the exact capabilities that determine whether a person can use AI tools effectively.
AI tools are language interfaces. Every interaction with a language model is a verbal task. Prompting is writing. Evaluating output is reading comprehension under ambiguity. Building workflows with AI requires abstraction — seeing the general pattern behind a specific task and encoding it. Directing an AI agent requires the ability to decompose a complex goal into component instructions. These are all verbal-cognitive operations.
What This Means for the Results Page
A higher Turing Vault score does not just mean the person is better at detecting AI. It means:
First, they are more likely to be effective prompters — prompting is a verbal skill, and higher verbal fluency produces more precise, more creative, and more effective prompts.
Second, they are better equipped to evaluate AI output — discrimination is the skill the test measures directly, and it transfers to every context where a person needs to decide whether AI output is good enough to use.
Third, they have higher creative upside with AI tools — verbal fluency correlates with the ability to see non-obvious applications, recombine ideas, and use tools in ways the tool designer did not anticipate.
Fourth, they are more capable of building complex AI workflows — abstraction, decomposition, and systems thinking correlate with verbal-cognitive performance.
The Resource Ladder Follows This Logic
The resource system is not arranged by difficulty for its own sake. It is calibrated to capability. A person scoring just above chance needs concrete, visual, step-by-step resources because that is what their verbal processing style can act on. A person scoring at 90 gets API documentation and orchestration architecture because they have the cognitive infrastructure to use it. The ladder is not condescending. It is functional. Giving someone a resource they cannot act on is not helpful — it is noise.
This is also why the risk framework and the resource system reinforce each other. Lower verbal fluency means higher exposure to AI disruption AND lower capability to adapt using AI tools. Higher verbal fluency means lower exposure AND higher capability to capitalize. The gap widens in both directions. The results page makes this visible.
Important Caveat — What We Are Not Measuring
Verbal fluency is not the whole picture. Discipline, executive function, sustained focus, and the willingness to sit with discomfort while learning something new all determine whether capability translates to outcomes. The Turing Vault does not measure any of these. A person scoring 95 with no follow-through will be outperformed by a person scoring 65 who executes every day for six months. The results page should never imply that score alone determines outcome. Score determines ceiling. Behavior determines whether the ceiling is reached.

LANDING PAGE APPLICATIONS
Several elements from this system should migrate to the landing page. The results page is the payoff. The landing page is the hook. The hook needs to communicate what the person will learn if they take the test.
Elements That Belong on the Landing Page
1. The Core Question
"How likely is AI to take your job — and on what timeframe?" This is the single strongest hook in the system. It should be visible above the fold. Not buried in subtext. Not softened. The question itself creates the impulse to take the test.
2. The Verbal Fluency Thesis (Condensed)
A 2-3 sentence version: "Your ability to distinguish AI from human text is a proxy for how you will navigate the AI economy. Higher verbal fluency means you are more likely to use AI tools effectively — and less likely to be replaced by them. The Turing Vault measures where you stand." This gives the test scientific grounding without requiring the full thesis.
3. The Risk Spectrum Preview
Show the range of possible outcomes in compressed form. Not the full copy — just the labels and timeframes. Something like a vertical scale showing: CRITICAL EXPOSURE / 12-24 months at the bottom, up to NEGLIGIBLE / Not foreseeable at the top. The user sees the full range of where they might land. This creates both curiosity and urgency.
4. The Resource Promise
"Based on your score, you will receive a curated set of tools, guides, and resources calibrated to your verbal fluency level — from AI survival basics to advanced builder-operator material." This tells the person the results page is useful, not just a number. It converts the test from entertainment to utility.
5. The Toggle Teaser
"Work with your hands? Stay-at-home parent? The assessment accounts for that." One line. Tells blue-collar and homemaker visitors they are not going to get an irrelevant result. Expands the addressable audience without diluting the core proposition.
What Does NOT Belong on the Landing Page
The full score-band copy. The detailed risk explanations. The resource links themselves. The implementation details. The caveat about executive function. None of these belong on the landing page because they are the product. The landing page sells the product. It does not deliver it.

PART 4 — RESOURCE ARCHITECTURE (CORRECTED + VERBAL FLUENCY CALIBRATION)
4.1 Design Principles (Updated)
Each score band maps to a resource tier. The tiers are calibrated to verbal fluency, not just score. This is the key change from v1. The question driving the resource ladder is not "what difficulty level matches this score?" The question is: "Given this person's demonstrated verbal fluency, what type of resource can they actually act on?"
A person with low verbal fluency needs visual, concrete, step-by-step material. Abstract concepts must be anchored to specific examples. A person with high verbal fluency can process raw documentation, code-adjacent tutorials, and strategic frameworks. Giving either group the other's resources is a waste. The ladder exists because capability determines utility.

User Goal: Understand what AI is, what it can do, and how to start using it to protect their employability.

User Mindset: "I don't understand what's happening and I don't know where to start."
